## Illness and Low Energy

## 1. When this applies
- Illness symptoms, unusual fatigue, low energy, poor recovery, or unclear "off" state
- Athlete is deciding whether to train, reduce, or resume

## 2. Primary coaching goals
- Do not turn a minor issue into a deeper setback
- Protect recovery first, fitness second
- Resume only from clear stability

## 3. What to prioritize
- Current symptoms over calendar or plan
- Distinguish: systemic illness vs local soreness vs normal tiredness
- Downshift early when signals are mixed
- Simple, low-cost decisions the athlete can follow

## 4. What to avoid
- Do not prescribe intensity through illness or heavy fatigue
- Do not treat motivation as readiness
- Do not "sweat it out"
- Do not use one better day as clearance
- Do not resume with both volume and intensity

## 5. Progression rules
- If systemic illness is active: rest or very light movement only
- If symptoms are improving but energy is unstable: easy only
- Require 24-48h of clear improvement before normal easy training
- Require 7 days of stable training before intensity
- First good day is observation, not progression
- Progress one lever at a time: frequency -> duration -> intensity

## 6. Red flags
- Fever, chest symptoms, dizziness, GI disruption, unusual shortness of breath
- Fatigue that is worse the next morning
- Symptoms that return during warm-up
- "I feel mostly fine now" after several bad days
- Athlete wants to catch up immediately

## 7. Example good response logic
- Active illness or unstable energy means reduce now, not later. Use rest or very easy work until symptoms and energy are clearly improving, then rebuild easy training first. Delay intensity until the athlete is stable again.

## 8. Example bad response logic
- "You missed a few days, so let's get a quality session in while you feel okay."
- "If it stays easy, a long run should be fine."
- "You sound motivated again, so normal training can resume."
