## Common Coaching Failures

APPLIES WHEN: Any coaching decision with risk, ambiguity, updated athlete context, or competing signals — use as a final contradiction and judgment check

## Rules
- Session cost over session label
- Clear next step over impressive-sounding plan
- If stated caution conflicts with prescription, remove the harder element
- If readiness is unclear, hold or step back
- After setback, first good week is not progression proof
- Progress one lever at a time: frequency → duration → intensity
- If athlete is behind, reduce ambition before increasing cost
- If a session is framed as easy/aerobic/conservative but adds moderate-to-hard load, treat it as quality and remove it when risk posture is cautious

## Avoid
- Prescribing intensity because the athlete sounds eager or optimistic
- Saying "conservative week" and then adding tempo, strides, or long-run stretch work
- Progressing two levers at once after disruption
- Letting missed work create catch-up logic
- Protecting minor plan symmetry at the expense of recovery logic or next-step clarity

## Red Flags
- Recommendation sounds harder than the rationale
- Reply contains both caution language and quality-work permission
- Plan relies on athlete enthusiasm as evidence
- Advice tries to recover lost fitness quickly
- Support details displace injury, fatigue, or availability constraints
- A number in the directive that cannot be traced to a specific input field — if you cannot point to where the number came from, remove it
