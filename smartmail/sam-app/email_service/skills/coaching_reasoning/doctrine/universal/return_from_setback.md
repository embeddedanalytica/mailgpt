## Return From Setback

### 1. When this applies

Use when training is resuming after pain flare, illness, fatigue spike, disrupted training, or an unstable block. This is the fragile rebuild phase, not normal build.

### 2. Primary coaching goals

Restore consistency. Prevent re-aggravation. Rebuild confidence in the body. Keep the athlete moving forward without needing a perfect week.

### 3. What to prioritize

Current athlete report over prior plan and stale memory. If the inherited plan is more aggressive than the athlete's current status supports, coach the safer interpretation rather than preserving the old progression. Repeatable completion over ambitious sessions. Low-cost work that protects rhythm. Clear statement of what is improving, what is still uncertain, and what must be true before progression.

### 4. What to avoid

Do not treat one good session or one good week as clearance. Do not prescribe intensity because the athlete sounds eager. Do not jump back to the old plan. Do not call the week conservative and then hide quality inside it. Do not progress volume and intensity together.

### 5. Progression rules

If this is the first good week after yellow/red signals, keep the week conservative.

If symptoms are still present during warm-up, during the session, or the next morning, progress duration before intensity.

If fewer than 7-10 stable days have been completed, do not add meaningful intensity. Stable days means training days completed without symptom worsening during the session or the next morning, and without the week starting to unravel.

If the athlete wants to make up lost training, treat that as a risk signal.

Progress one lever at a time: consistency -> low-risk volume -> specificity -> intensity.

Only reintroduce intensity when completion is stable, symptoms are not worsening, and one bad day would not derail the week. Meaningful intensity includes tempo, threshold, intervals, hills, strides used as quality, fast finishes, or any session meant to add moderate-to-hard load rather than simply preserve easy rhythm.

### 6. Red flags

Catch-up language. Testing language. Recent good-day/bad-day oscillation. Pain that warms up then returns. Fatigue that feels better mid-session but worsens the next day. Cautious wording paired with aggressive prescription.

### 7. Example good response logic

Acknowledge improvement without over-reading it. Name the phase as fragile. Keep the next step easy enough to repeat. Hold the line if the athlete wants to push. Preserve rhythm before chasing fitness. If the old plan called for a harder session, explicitly downshift it rather than quietly carrying it forward. A good progression sequence is: complete the week cleanly, repeat the structure, then add small volume or sport-specific work before adding real intensity.

### 8. Example bad response logic

"You felt good this week, so let's resume tempo work."

"The pain settled during the run, so strides are fine."

"You only missed a few sessions, so let's make up the long run."

"We'll stay aerobic this week" followed by tempo, hills, fast finish, or multiple progression steps.
