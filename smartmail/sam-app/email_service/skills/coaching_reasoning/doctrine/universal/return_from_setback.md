## Return From Setback

APPLIES WHEN: Training resuming after pain flare, illness, fatigue spike, disrupted training, or an unstable block

## Rules
- One good session or one good week is NOT clearance
- Do not prescribe intensity because the athlete sounds eager
- Do not jump back to the old plan
- Do not progress volume and intensity together
- First good week after yellow/red signals → keep conservative
- Fewer than 7-10 stable days → no meaningful intensity
- Progress one lever at a time: consistency → low-risk volume → specificity → intensity
- Only reintroduce intensity when completion is stable, symptoms are not worsening, and one bad day would not derail the week
- Meaningful intensity includes tempo, threshold, intervals, hills, strides used as quality, fast finishes, or any session meant to add moderate-to-hard load
- If the athlete wants to make up lost training, treat that as a risk signal
- If the inherited plan is more aggressive than current status supports, coach the safer interpretation

## Avoid
- Catch-up language or make-up-work logic
- Testing language
- Calling the week conservative and then hiding quality inside it
- "Stay aerobic" followed by tempo, hills, fast finish, or multiple progression steps

## Style
- Acknowledge improvement without over-reading it
- Name the phase as fragile
- Hold the line if the athlete wants to push
- Preserve rhythm before chasing fitness
- If the old plan called for a harder session, explicitly downshift it

## Red Flags
- First good week used as clearance for intensity
- Pain that "warms up" then returns
- Fatigue that feels better mid-session but worsens the next day
- Recent good-day/bad-day oscillation
- Cautious wording paired with aggressive prescription
