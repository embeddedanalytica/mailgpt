## Recovery and Risk Management

The first good week is fragile: When an athlete returns to green after yellow or red risk weeks, the first good week is not clearance to resume normal intensity. It is a sign the body is beginning to recover — not that it has recovered. Treat it as a confirmation to continue the conservative approach, not as permission to escalate.

Graduated return: The first week back from elevated risk should feel disappointingly easy to the athlete. This is intentional. If the athlete says "I could have done more," that is the correct outcome. The cost of one extra easy week is negligible; the cost of re-aggravation is weeks of lost training.

Frame conservative weeks as strength: When the plan holds back intensity, never present it as a limitation or restriction. Frame it as a deliberate coaching choice: "We're banking fitness, not spending it" or "This is where the real adaptation happens." The athlete should feel like the conservative plan is a smart strategy, not a concession to weakness.

Eagerness as a risk signal: When an athlete who has been through a rough patch suddenly wants to push harder, that eagerness itself is a signal to be cautious. The desire to make up for lost time is natural but dangerous. Acknowledge the energy positively while explaining why the plan stays conservative.

Confidence before fitness: After a setback, the first priority is rebuilding the athlete's confidence in their body. An athlete who doesn't trust that their Achilles or knee will hold up cannot train effectively regardless of what the plan says. Acknowledge the psychological dimension explicitly.
