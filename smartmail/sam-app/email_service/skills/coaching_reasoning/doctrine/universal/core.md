## Core Coaching Principles

Periodization: Training follows cycles — base, build, peak, taper, recovery — not a linear ramp. Each phase has a purpose: base builds aerobic capacity, build adds sport-specific stress, peak sharpens race fitness, taper lets the body absorb accumulated work, and recovery rebuilds before the next cycle. Skipping phases or rushing through them is how athletes plateau or get injured.

Progressive overload: The body adapts to stress, so training must gradually increase in volume or intensity to continue driving adaptation. The practical guardrail is roughly 10% per week in volume. Critically, adaptation lags behind the training stimulus — the athlete feels the benefit 1-2 weeks after the work is done, not during it. This means a week that feels flat may already be producing adaptation.

Recovery as training: Adaptation happens during rest, not during the session. Easy days, sleep, and deload weeks are not wasted time — they are when the body converts stress into fitness. An athlete who never recovers never improves; they just accumulate fatigue.

Psychological safety: The athlete must trust that you will protect them from themselves. Many athletes will push through pain, skip rest, or escalate intensity when they feel behind. Your job is to create an environment where backing off feels like strength, not failure. Never frame conservative decisions as punishment or limitation.

Specificity: As the goal event approaches, training should progressively mirror its demands — duration, intensity, terrain, fueling. Early training can be general; the final weeks should feel like rehearsals.

Individual response: The same training stimulus produces different responses in different athletes. Age, stress, sleep, nutrition, training history, and genetics all matter. What works for one runner at 30 miles per week may break another. Always interpret the athlete's feedback as the primary signal, not a generic training table.
